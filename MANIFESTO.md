# Cartelera — Manifesto

Cartelera is a guide to what is happening in your city, right now, that is worth
leaving the house for. It begins in Barcelona.

## The problem we are addressing

Cities are full of life, and most people miss almost all of it. Not for lack of
events — for lack of a way in. The information exists, scattered across venue
sites, municipal calendars, ticketing platforms, and Instagram pages, but it is
either a firehose (everything, ranked by who paid) or a trickle (one curator's
narrow taste). Neither helps the person sitting at home on a Tuesday evening who
would happily go *do something* if something caught their attention.

That person is the heart of this project. We are living through a loneliness
epidemic, and one of its quiet mechanisms is friction: it has become easier to
stay in than to go out, easier to scroll than to show up. A previous century had
habits we have lost — going to your usual spot and finding familiar faces,
stumbling into things, treating the city as a place you inhabit rather than a
backdrop you pass through. We believe software can help rebuild those habits
instead of replacing them.

Cartelera is not a feed. It is not trying to maximize the time you spend looking
at it. It succeeds when you close it and go outside.

## What we are building

A curated, navigable guide to a city's cultural life — small enough to trust,
broad enough to matter. We start with the categories that are genuinely
underserved and genuinely worth showing up for, and we earn trust by being
excellent in a narrow band before we are comprehensive across a wide one.

## Principles

These are decision-making references. When a choice is unclear, we resolve it
toward these.

**1. The real world over the feed.**
Every design decision is judged by one question: does this get someone out the
door, into a room, among other people? Engagement *with the app* is not a goal
and is often a warning sign. If a feature makes the app stickier but does not
make the city more reachable, we do not build it.

**2. Tonight over someday.**
The default frame is the present and the near future — what can I walk into in
the next few hours or days. Long-horizon planning is a secondary mode, not the
front door. A month-grid calendar is a tool for organizers; a "what's on right
now" list is a tool for living. We optimize for the latter.

**3. Curation is trust, and trust is earned at the source.**
We do not aggregate everything and sort by popularity. We choose which venues
and sources are worth following, and we stand behind them. A place on Cartelera
is an implicit recommendation. This means we grow deliberately, we say no often,
and we treat the act of *choosing what to include* as the core editorial work —
not an afterthought to scraping.

**4. Places, not transactions.**
The atomic unit of the city is the venue — a place with an identity, a regular
crowd, a character — not the individual ticketed event. Events come and go;
places are where belonging accumulates. We design venue-first so that the
product can grow toward familiarity and regulars, not just toward a longer list
of things to buy tickets to.

**5. Low stakes for the solo go-er.**
Going out alone should never feel like a failure mode. We surface what lowers the
barrier — free entry, drop-in nights, recurring regulars' events — and we keep
our tone such that no event reads as requiring a group. The person going alone is
a first-class user, not an edge case.

**6. Belonging over reach.**
Our long-term bet is not on being the biggest listings site. It is on rebuilding,
in a form native to this century, the lost habits of showing up, recognizing
faces, and having a place that is *yours*. Recurring nights, usual spots, and
(eventually) the people you go with matter more than raw coverage. When forced to
choose between scale and depth of belonging, we choose belonging.

## What this is not (yet), but is built toward

We are deliberately starting simple: a fast, static, account-free guide. But the
foundations are laid so the project can grow toward its real ambition without
being rebuilt — toward usual spots and regulars, toward going with friends,
toward (someday) hardware and signals that gently prompt you to step outside when
your city has something to offer. None of this is the first release. All of it is
the direction.

The polestar: **a tool that helps people live more of their life in the real
world.**
